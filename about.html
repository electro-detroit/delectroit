About Delectroit

An interactive map documenting sound-art venues, installations, and related places across Detroit.

Overview

Delectroit is a small mapping project that highlights locations connected to Detroit's sound-art community. Use the map to explore venues and public installations, click markers for details, and use the Menu to zoom or fit all markers.

Narrative

Across Detroit, each repurposed venue becomes a layered soundscape where history, architecture, and community practice converge. When Hart Plaza opened in 1975, its riverfront concrete transformed a proposed civic centre into a civic sound field, highlighting how scholars describe “historical soundscapes” as archives of collective memory that reveal urban identities. Similarly, the Marble Bar’s shift from an LGBTQ+ leather bar to a live‑music venue illustrates how “soundmarks of the past” are re‑inscribed, a process identified in studies of Castilian cities where normative sound regulations once shaped spatial identity. 

Spot Lite Detroit, launched by a first‑generation immigrant, mirrors the “multi‑sensorial stimulus” approach used in Jakarta’s Fatahillah Square analysis, where soundwalks and recalled memories redefine historic places. Tangent Gallery & Hastings Street Ballroom’s endurance as an avant‑garde hub reflects the “sociocultural construction of soundscape” noted in Pompeii’s theatres, where architectural form dictates acoustic experience. 

The Lincoln Factory’s conversion from auto production to a music venue exemplifies the “place‑making” strategy outlined for industrial heritage sites, where soundscape design links tangible and intangible heritage. Research on historic districts shows that intentional sound selection, control, and “sensing routes” can restore a “historical sense” and authenticity, countering luxury‑condo onversion. Together, these narratives demonstrate that urban venues are not static shells or sites but evolving acoustic commons, where sound deliberately reshapes space, memory, and social belonging.

Data Used

Scholarly Resources: Academic articles and research papers provided theoretical frameworks and historical context for understanding Detroit's sonic spaces and helping build our narrative.

Venue Documentation: The official websites of venues and locations offered operational details, programming information, and institutional histories.

Public Data: We utilized datasets from Detroit Data (detroitdata.org) to identify and verify location information after making our decision to focus on Detroit as our geographic area of study.

Methodology

Framework: Bootstrap, OpenStreetMap contributors.

Library: Leaflet.js enabled interactive mapping features

Division of Labor: Each team member researched, coded, and documented five locations

Navigation Structure: We created an integrated site architecture including:

  • Interactive map interface (main page)
  • About section (project context and objectives)
  • Teams page (contributor information)
  • Unified navigation bar linking all sections
